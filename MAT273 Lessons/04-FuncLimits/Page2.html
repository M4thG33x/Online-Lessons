Limits & Continuity

Back in Calc 1, the first real step into calculus topics begins with the study of limits and continuity. Pretty much everything you learned back then can also be applied to functions of multiple variables. With functions of 1-variable, we consider limits as \(x\) approaches some number \(a\), or \(x \rightarrow a\). To determine if a limit existed, we often had to look at the left and right limits, or \(x \rightarrow a^{-}\) and \(x \rightarrow a^{+}\).

With functions of two variables, we must consider limits as \((x,y)\) points in the domain approach a specific location, i.e., \((x,y) \rightarrow (a,b)\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers and \((a,b)\) is in the domain of \(f\). And instead of left and right limits, we must consider any path that could be taken to get to the point \((a,b)\). For the limit to exist, the limit along ALL possible paths must be the same.

This leads us to a test to show that a limit does NOT exist, which we can call the Two Path Test. If we can find two paths that go through the point (a, b) where their limits are different, then we can conclude that the limit as a whole does not exist.

Continuity

The intuitive idea of continuity of a function \(y = f(x)\) back in Calc 1 was that the graph of a function must be one single curve with no breaks, jumps or gaps in order to be continuous. If you could draw the graph of a function in one motion without lifting your pen then it was continuous. Any break, jump or gap was called a point of discontinuity. This same idea is true for a function of multiple variables. It will be continuous everywhere on its domain except where there are breaks or holes.

Here is a more formal definition of continuity.

A function \(f(x,y)\) is continuous at a point \((a,b)\) if the following limit exists and is equal to the value of \(f\) at \((a,b)\).

\[\lim_{(x,y) \to (a,b)} f(x,y) = f(a,b)\]

The following three criteria for continuity of a function \(f(x,y)\) at a point \((a,b)\) come from the definition of continuity in Calc 1.

  1. \(f(a,b)\) exists.
  2. The limit as \((x,y) \rightarrow (a,b)\) exists.
  3. The limit is equal to \(f(a,b)\).
