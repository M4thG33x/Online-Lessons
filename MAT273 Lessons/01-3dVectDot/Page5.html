Dot Product

In the previous lesson, we looked at the basic vector operations of addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication. In this lesson, we will look at a way to multipliy vectors. We will see a second method in the next lesson.

The vector dot product is the most obvious method of multipying vectors, just multiply the corresponding components like we did with addition and subtraction. However, there is a little twist. Once we multiply the components, we also add up these values. This means that a dot product results in a scalar quantity, not another vector.

We define the dot product of two vectors \(\vec{u} = \langle u_1,u_2,u_3 \rangle \) and \(\vec{v} = \langle v_1,v_2,v_3 \rangle \) as follows.

\[\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + u_3 v_3 \]

The dot product has the following properties given vectors \(\vec{u}\), \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\) and a scalar \(c\).

Properties of the Dot Product
Property Operation
Commutative \[\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = \vec{v} \cdot \vec{u}\]
Associative (scalar) \[c(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}) = (c\vec{u}) \cdot \vec{v}\] or \[c(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}) = \vec{u} \cdot (c\vec{v})\]
Distributive (vector) \[\vec{u} \cdot (\vec{v} + \vec{w}) = \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} + \vec{u} \cdot \vec{w}\]
Magnitude \[\vec{u} \cdot \vec{u} = |\vec{u}|^2\]

To understand what the dot product represents, we can consider the following formulas as an alternative to the original definition.

If \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are two vectors and \(\theta\) is the angle between them, where \(0 \le \theta \le \pi\), then we can define the vector dot product using the following equation.

\[\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}| |\vec{v}| \cos \theta\]

If the vectors \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are parallel and point in the same, then \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\). If the vectors are orthogonal (or perpendicular), then \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0\). And if the vectors are parallel but point in opposite directions, then \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = -|\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\). If the vectors are neither parallel nor perpendicular, then the value of the dot product will bounded by the product of their magnitudes. In other words, \(-|\vec{u}||\vec{v}| \le \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} \le |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\).

Using this alternate formula for the dot product, we get a few more properties for the dot product.

  • If \(\vec{u} = \vec{0}\) or \(\vec{v} = \vec{0}\), then \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0\) and \(\theta\) is undefined.
  • The vectors \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are parallel (meaning \(\theta = 0\) or \(\theta = \pi\) if and only if \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = \pm |\vec{u}||\vec{v}|\).
  • The vectors \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are perpendicular (meaning \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\)) if and only if \(\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0\).

Projections

Sometimes we need to know how much of one vector points in the direction of another vector. We call this an orthogonal projection of one vector onto another vector. For example, the project of any vector \(\vec{v} = \langle a,b \rangle\) onto the unit vector \(\hat{\imath}\) (which corresponds to the \(x\)-axis) would result in the component vector \(\langle a,0 \rangle\). Similarly, projecting \(\vec{v} = \langle a,b \rangle\) on the the unit vector \(\hat{\jmath}\) (the \(y\)-axis) would result in the component vector \(\langle 0,b \rangle\). But we can project a vector onto any vector, not just \(\hat{\imath}\) and \(\hat{\jmath}\).

In the above example, we see two vectors: \(\vec{u} = \langle 2,3 \rangle\) and \(\vec{v} = \langle 5,3 \rangle\). The red vector \(\vec{w}\) is the projection of \(\vec{u}\) onto \(\vec{v}\). It is almost like vector \(w\) is the component of \(\vec{u}\) in the direction of \(\vec{v}\). We would express this as \(\vec{w} = \text{proj}_{\vec{v}} \vec{u}\).

The orthogonal project of \(\vec{u}\) onto \(\vec{v}\) is given by the following formula.

\[\text{proj}_\vec{v} \vec{u} = \frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}}{\vec{v} \cdot \vec{v}} \vec{v}\]

Probably the most common application of the vector dot product deals with force and work. If you remember your physics, we can desribe the work done moving an object a distance \(d\) by applying a constant force \(F\) in the direction of the motion as \(W = Fd\). Well, what if the force is not applied in the same direction as the movement of the object? We would need to determine how much of the force is parallel to the direction of motion, which is where the projection comes into play.

The amount of work \(\vec{W}\) done by a force \(\vec{F}\) along a distance \(\vec{d}\) is given by the following dot product.

\[\vec{W} = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = \left| \text{proj}_{\vec{d}} \vec{F} \right| |\vec{d}|\]
