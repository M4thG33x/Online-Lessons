Stoke's Theorem

Earlier in this unit, we learned how to evaluate line integrals of a vector field \(\vec{F}\) over a curve \(C\). We then saw that according to Green's Theorem, we could relate the integral over \(C\) to the area \(D\) bounded inside \(C\). In other words, Green's Theorem tells us that the net circulation along \(C\) is equal to the net rotation in \(D\). We can now extend this idea of line integrals and bounded regions in the \(xy\)-plane to surface integrals in space.

Let \(S\) be an oriented piecewise-smooth surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) bounded by a simple, closed, piecewise-smooth curve \(C\) with positive orientation. Let \(\vec{F}\) be a vector field whose components have continuous partial first derivatives on \(S\). Then Stoke's Theorem says the line integral over \(C\) is equal to the curl across \(S\).

\[\underset{C}{\mathop \oint}\, \vec{F} \cdot~d\vec{r} = \underset{E}{\mathop \iint}\, \text{curl}\, \vec{F} \cdot~d\vec{S}\]

If \(\vec{n}\) is the unit vector normal to \(S\) in the direction of the positive orientation, then we can rewrite this as follows.

\[\underset{C}{\mathop \int}\, \vec{F} \cdot~d\vec{r} = \underset{S}{\mathop \iint}\, (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot \vec{n}~dS\]

Notice that this is similar to the flux integral of \(\vec{F}\) across \(S\) that we saw in the previous lessons. So we could think of Stokes' Theorem as equating the line integral over the boundary \(C\) with the flux of the curl of \(\vec{F}\) over the surface \(S\). As we saw in the last lesson, if \(S\) can be expressed explicitlly as \(z = g(x, y)\) where \(g_x\) and \(g_y\) are continuous over the region \(D\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), then we could also rewrite Green's Theorem as the integral below.

\[\underset{C}{\mathop \int}\, \vec{F} \cdot~d\vec{r} = \underset{S}{\mathop \iint}\, (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot \langle -g_x,-g_y,1 \rangle ~dA\]

Stokes' Theorem tells us that the net circulation along the boundary curve is equal to the net rotation over the surface.
