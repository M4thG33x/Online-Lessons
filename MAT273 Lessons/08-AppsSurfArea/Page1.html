Applications of Double Integrals

Now let's spend a few minutes looking at some applications of double integrals. Some of these will be similar to applications you would have seen in Calc II (see chapter 8). For example, back in Calc II we compute the center of mass of a thin plate, or a lamina, that has a uniform density. Now we want to consider the mass with a variable density.

For a lamina region \(D\) in the \(xy\)-plane with density function \(\rho(x, y)\), the total mass of the lamina is given by the following formula. Note that \(\rho\) is the Greek letter rho.

\[m=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,\rho \left( x,y \right)~dA\]

Center of Mass

You could think of the center of mass of a lamina object as the point on the lamina where the object would be perfectly balance, like the fulcrum point of a seesaw or balance scale. The moments of the lamina object, \(M_x\) and \(M_y\), measure the tendancy of the object to rotate about the \(x\) or \(y\) axis, respectively.

For a lamina region \(D\) in the \(xy\)-plane with mass \(m\) and density \(\rho(x, y)\), we can compute the moments about the \(x\)-axis and \(y\)-axis as well as the center of mass of the lamina.

\[{{M}_{x}}=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,y~\rho \left( x,y \right)~dA\] \[{{M}_{y}}=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,x~\rho \left( x,y \right)~dA\] \[\left( \bar{x},\bar{y} \right)=\left( \frac{{{M}_{y}}}{m},\frac{{{M}_{x}}}{m} \right)\]

[https://youtu.be/wvLriBilXb4]

Moment of Inertia

Another physics application of \(x\) double integrals involves moment of inertia, which measures the tendency of an object to resist angular acceleration. If you have a particle mass \(m\), then its moment of inertia about an axis is \(I=mr^2\) where \(m\) is the mass and \(r\) is the distance of the partical from the axis. For a lamina object, we must add up the moments of inertia for all points in the lamina, about both the \(x\) and \(y\) axes.

For a lamina region \(D\) in the \(xy\)-plane with mass \(m\) and density \(\rho(x, y)\), we can compute the moments of inertia about the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis, and the origin using the following formulas.

\[{{I}_{x}}=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,{{y}^{2}}\rho \left( x,y \right)~dA\] \[{{I}_{y}}=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,{{x}^{2}}\rho \left( x,y \right)~dA\] \[{{I}_{o}}=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,\left( {{x}^{2}}+{{y}^{2}} \right)\rho \left( x,y \right)~dA\]

The following video demonstrates how to compute \(I_x\) for a plane lamina. A similar process could be followed to get \(I_y\) and then \(I_o=I_x+I_y\).

Probability & Expected Value

The last application we will consider for now is computing the probability of an event happening that depends on two independent variables. For starters, a probability density function of one independent variable is a function \(f\) of a continuous random variable \(X\) such that \(f(x)≥0\) for all \(x\) and \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\,f(x)~dx = 1\). The probability that \(X\) lies between values \(a\) and \(b\) is found by \[P(a \leq X \leq b)=\int_{a}^{b}\,f(x)~dx.\] A common example is the exponential density function below, where \(\mu\) is the mean (or average value) of the probability distribution. \(f(x)=\frac{1}{\mu}e^{-X/\mu}\). Note \(\mu\) is the Greek letter mu, which is often used to represent the mean.

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are two random continuous variables, then the joint density function of \(X\) and \(Y\) is a function of two variables, \(f(x,y)\). The probability that both \(X\) and \(Y\) are within specific ranges can be described as the point \((X, Y)\) being with a region \(D\) of the \(xy\)-plane that corresponds to the specified ranges, which is given by \[P((X,Y) \in D)=\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,f(x,y)~dA.\] Because \(f\) is still a probability density function, both \(f(x,y) \geq 0\) and \(\underset{D}{\mathop \iint }\,f(x,y)~dA=1\) are still true.

Lastly, we say \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent random variables if \(X\) has a probability density function \(f_1(x)\) and \(Y\) has a probability density function \(f_2(y)\) where \(f(x,y)=f_1(x)f_2(y)\). In other words, the joint density function is the product of their individual probability density functions.

Find a Probability Using the Exponential Density Function
[https://youtu.be/PvfFDavUKOc]

The expected value of \(X\) is the mean value of \(X\), and the expected value of \(Y\) is the mean value of \(Y\). We can compute the expected values of \(X\) and \(Y\), \(\mu_x\) and \(\mu_y\) respectively, using the following formulas.

\[\mu_x=\underset{R}{\mathop \iint }\,x\,f(x,y)~dA\] \[\mu_y=\underset{R}{\mathop \iint }\,y\,f(x,y)~dA\]
