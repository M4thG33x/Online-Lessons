Curl

In the previous lesson, we introduced the idea of the curl of a 2D vector field (in the \(xy\)-plane). Now we want to extend this to the curl in 3D, and we want to talk about something called the divergence of a vector field. However, before diving into curl and divergence of 3D vector fields, let's look at them a bit more in just the \(xy\)-plane. The following video gives wonderful visualizations and interpretations for the curl and divergence of vector fields. We will get to the actual computations a bit later.

Divergence and curl: The language of Maxwell's equations, fluid flow, and more
[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rB83DpBJQsE]

So the curl of a vector field describes the rotation or spinning effect of the field. The curl can be 0, which means there is no rotation, i.e. it is irrotational, or it could have a positive or negative curl. Curl is a vector whose magnitude is the strength of the rotation and whose direction is perpendicular to the rotation. We can determine this direction using the right hand rule - curve your fingers of your right hand in the direction of the rotation and your thumb will point in the direction of the curl vector. This should remind you of the vector cross product that we learned about back in unit 1, especially since we compute the curl of a vector field using a cross product.

The curl of a vector field \(\vec{F} = \langle P,Q,R \rangle \) on \(ℝ^3\) where \(P\), \(Q\) and \(R\) are differentiable on \(ℝ^3\) is defined by the following formula.

\[\begin{align*} \text{curl} \,\vec{F} = \nabla \times \vec{F} &= \begin{bmatrix} \hat{\imath} & \hat{\jmath} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ P & Q & R \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\frac{\partial R}{\partial x},\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \rangle \end{align*}\]

If \(\nabla \times \vec{F} = \vec{0}\), then \(\vec{F}\) is irrotational.
