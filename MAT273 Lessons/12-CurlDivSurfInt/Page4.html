Tangent Plane

One nice usage of these surface parameterizations in the ability to determine the equation of the tangent plane to a surface at a point. Recall from unit 1 that the equation of a plane is given by \(a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0\) where\(\langle a,b, c \rangle\) is the normal vector at the point \((x_0, y_0, z_0)\). Well for a parameterized surface \(S\) given by \(\vec{r}(u,v) = \langle x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v) \rangle\), the normal vector will be perpendicular to the partial derivatives \(\vec{r}_u\) and \(\vec{r}_v\).

\[\vec{n} = \langle a,b,c \rangle = \vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_v\]

Example:

Find the tangent plane to the paraboloid surface \(S\) given by \(z = x^2 + y^2 + 1\) at the point \((1, -1, 3)\).

If we let \(u=x\) and \(v=y\), then we could easily parameterize \(S\) as \(\vec{r}(u,v) = \langle u,v,u^2 + v^2 + 1 \rangle\), and the partial derivatives would then be \(\vec{r}_u = \langle 1,0,2u \rangle\) and \(\vec{r}_v = \langle 0,1,2v \rangle\). At the given point, where \(u = x = 1\) and \(v = y = -1\), we get the vectors \(\vec{r}_u = \langle 1,0,2 \rangle\) and \(\vec{r}_v = \langle 0,1,-2 \rangle\). The normal vector would then be \(\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_v = \langle -2,2,1 \rangle\). Thus the equation of the plane would be \(-2(x - 1) + 2(y + 1) +1(z - 3) = 0\), or \(2x - 2y - z = 1\).
