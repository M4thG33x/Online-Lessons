Chain Rule

Back in Calc 1, you learned about the chain rule, which said the derivative of a function \(y = f(u)\) where \(u = g(x)\) would be \(y' = f'(u) \cdot u' = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\). Using the \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) notation, we could express this as follows.

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\]

Now, we want to consider the chain rule for multiple variables.

Let's start by looking at the derivative of a function with two variables that share one independent variable. Using the following formula, we can determine the derivative of \(z = f(x,y)\) where \(x = g(t)\) and \(y = h(t)\) and \(t\) is a real number.

\[\frac{df}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{dy}{dt}\]

Note that we must determine the partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\) while also determining the regular derivatives of \(x\) and \(y\) with respect to \(t\).

The Chain Rule for Functions of Two Variable with One Independent Variable
[https://youtu.be/KTD4Ire3VDY]

Next, we want to consider the derivative of a function with two variables that share two independent variable. Using the following formulas, we can determine the derivative of \(z = f(x,y)\) where \(x = g(s,t)\) and \(y = h(s,t)\) where \(s\) and \(t\) are real numbers using the formulas

\[\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \cdot \frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial s}\]\[\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \cdot \frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\]

Note that we must determine the partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\) while also determining the partial derivatives of \(x\) and \(y\) with respect to \(s\) and \(t\).

Implicit Differentiation

We often work with expressions that define \(f\) explicitly as a function of \(x\) and \(y\), i.e., \(z = f(x,y)\). But just like we did in Calc 1, we can also work with functions defined implicitly.

If we are given an equation \(F(x,y) = 0\) that defines \(y\) implicitly as a function of \(x\), then the differentiation process can be simplified to the following formula, where \(F_x\) and \(F_y\) are the partial derivatives of \(F\).

\[\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}\]
