CrossProduct

Relatively speaking, the cross product is a bit more difficult to compute compared to the dot project. For one thing, the result of a cross product is a vector, not a scalar like the dot product. It is also going to involve a little bit of linear algebra, namely determinants of 2x2 and 3x3 matrices. A matrix is just a collection of numbers organized into rows and columns. So a 2x2 matrix has 2 rows and 2 columns, such as the matrix below.

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\]

The determinant of a 2x2 matrix is a scalar computed using the following formula.

\[\left| \begin{matrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{matrix} \right| = ad - bc\]

Self-Check #1: Compute the following determinant.

\[\left| \begin{matrix} 7 & 5 \\ -2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right|\]
You must enter an answer.

\[\left| \begin{matrix} 7 & 5 \\ -2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right| = (7)(1) - (5)(-2) = 7 + 10 = 17\]

The easy way to remember this is that you are just multiplying the diagonal values and subtracting the results. The following video gives a couple examples. (You can ignore any of the other linear algebra stuff in the video.)

The cross product of vectors \(\vec{u} = \langle u_1,u_2,u_3 \rangle\) and \(\vec{v} = \langle v_1,v_2,v_3 \rangle\) is defined by the following formula.

\[\begin{align*} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} &= \left| \begin{matrix} \hat{\imath} & \hat{\jmath} & \hat{k} \\ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{matrix} \right| \\ &= \left| \begin{matrix} u_2 & u_3 \\ v_2 & v_3 \end{matrix} \right| \hat{\imath} - \left| \begin{matrix} u_1 & u_3 \\ v_1 & v_3 \end{matrix} \right| \hat{\jmath} + \left| \begin{matrix} u_1 & u_2 \\ v_1 & v_2 \end{matrix} \right| \hat{k} \\ &= \langle u_2 v_3 - u_3 v_2, -(u_1 v_3 - u_3 v_1), u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1 \rangle \end{align*}\]

The Right-Hand Rule: If you point the first finger of your right hand in the direction of \(\vec{u}\) and the second finger of your right hand in the direction of \(\vec{v}\), then extending your thumb will point in the direction of \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}\).

The first row of the 3x3 matrix will always be \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\) and \(\hat{k}\). The middle row will always be the first vector of the \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}\) notation, and the bottom row will always be the second vector.

Let's review some of the properties of the cross product stated in the video above.

  1. \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}\) is a vector that is orthogonal (perpendicular) to both \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\).
  2. \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v} = -(\vec{v} \times \vec{u})\), which is the anticommutative property.
  3. \(|\vec{u} \times \vec{v}| = |\vec{u} |\vec{v}| \sin \theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\).
  4. \(\vec{u} \times \vec{v} = \vec{0}\) (zero vector) if and only if \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are scalar multiples of each other, i.e. \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) are parallel.
  5. \(|\vec{u} \times \vec{v}|\) equals the area of the parallelogram formed by adjacent sides \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\).

Self-Check #2: What is the area of the parallelogram whose opposite sides correspond to the vectors \(\vec{u} = \langle 4,-1,2 \rangle\) and \(\vec{v} = \langle 5,3,-2 \rangle\)? Round your answer to 2 decimal places.

You must enter an answer.

Begin by computing the cross product of the given vectors.

\[\begin{align*} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} &= \left| \begin{matrix} \hat{\imath} & \hat{\jmath} & \hat{k} \\ 4 & -1 & 2 \\ 5 & 3 & -2 \end{matrix} \right| \\ &= \langle (-1)(-2) - (2)(3), -\left((4)(-2) - (2)(5)\right), (4)(3) - (-1)(5) \rangle \\ &= \langle 2-6, -(-8-10), 12+5 \rangle \\ &= \langle -4, 18, 17 \rangle \end{align*}\]

The area of the parallelogram formed by \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) is equal to the magnitude of their cross product.

\[\left|\vec{u} \times \vec{v} \right| = \sqrt{(-4)^2+(18)^2+(17)^2} = \sqrt{629} \approx 25.08\]
