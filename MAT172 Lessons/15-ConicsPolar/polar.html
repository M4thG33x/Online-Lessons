Polar Form

Let's shift our focus to looking at conic sections using polar coordinates. In particular, we want to consider parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas that have a focus at the origin. We have already discussed eccentricity for ellipses and the directrix for parabolas, but now we will see that all three conic shapes have an eccentricity and at least one directrix.

The equation of a conic section with a focus at the origin having an eccentricity \(e\) and directrix \(x = \pm d\) or \(y = \pm d\) will have one of the following polar forms.

\[r = \frac{de}{1 \pm e \cos{\theta}} \text{ or } r = \frac{de}{1 \pm e \sin{\theta}}\]

How does the eccentricity effect the shape of the graph? How can we tell when the conic will be a parabola, ellipse or hyperbola? Here are some things to remember.

  • The leading term in the denominator should always be a 1. If it is not a 1, then we need to simplify the expression to make it a 1.
  • The graph will be oriented vertically if the equation contains \(\sin{\theta}\) or horizontally if the equation contains \(\cos{\theta}\), which describes the direction of the axis of symmetry (for parabolas), major axis (for ellipses), or transverse axis (for hyperbolas).
  • The directrix will be perpendicular to the orientation of the graph, so a vertical conic will have a horizontal directrix \(y = \pm d\) while a horizontal conic will have a vertical directrix \(x = \pm d\).
  • Since each conic must curve away from the directrix and around the focus (at the origin), the sign of the directrix will match the sign of the denominator.
  • The polar equation represents an ellipse if \(e < 1\), a parabola if \(e = 1\), or a hyperobla if \(e > 1\).
